🎯 Discovery Questions By persona ▼
CISO / Security Leadership
Which AI tools are your teams using today? Do you have a way to track that centrally?
Do you have an AI usage policy? How is it enforced — technically or just on paper?
If a regulator asked for a log of AI interactions involving customer data from the last 90 days, could you produce it?
Where does AI fit in your security architecture — is it treated like any other SaaS app, or does it have its own controls?
SOC Manager / Security Operations
Are any of your security tools — SIEM, SOAR, or analyst workflows — using AI or LLM capabilities today?
If they are, do you have visibility into what data those tools send to external AI models?
How would you detect if an AI-assisted workflow produced a recommendation based on manipulated input?
VP Engineering / Platform
How many of your developers use AI coding assistants — Copilot, Cursor, or similar?
What code context gets sent with each completion? Have you reviewed what's included?
What's the process when an AI tool suggests code that introduces a vulnerability — would anyone catch it?
Compliance / GRC
Do any of your compliance frameworks — SOC 2, HIPAA, PCI-DSS — require controls around AI usage? How are you addressing that?
Do you have audit trails for AI interactions that touch regulated data?
For organizations with data residency requirements: are those enforced for AI API calls, or just for traditional data flows?
⚔️ Objection Playbook They say / You say ▼
"We're not really using AI that much."
That's worth validating. IBM found that 1 in 5 breaches now involve shadow AI — tools adopted without security's knowledge. The question isn't whether your teams are using AI. It's whether you have visibility into how much and what data is going in. Can you produce a report of every AI interaction from the last 90 days? If not, that's the gap worth exploring.
"We already have DLP for this."
DLP is a strong foundation — and endpoint DLP can absolutely see what users type into AI tools. The gap isn't visibility, it's context. DLP classifies data patterns, but it doesn't understand prompt structure. It can't detect a prompt injection hiding inside a document. It doesn't validate whether an AI response contains hallucinated data or insecure code before it reaches a downstream system. The proxy adds an AI-aware governance layer on top of what DLP already does well — they're complementary, not competing.
"Our AI provider says our data is safe."
Provider commitments matter — opt-out of training, data encryption, SOC 2 compliance. But those controls protect data at rest on the provider's side. They don't prevent an employee from putting sensitive data into a prompt in the first place. They don't detect prompt injection attacks. And they don't give you a centralized audit trail across multiple providers. The proxy is your governance layer — it works regardless of which provider or model your teams choose.
"This feels like it would slow down our developers."
Fair concern. No one wants to be the person who makes developers less productive. In practice, inline inspection adds low single-digit milliseconds — well under the variance developers already experience with API latency. The more honest conversation is about risk tolerance: what's the cost of a credential leak requiring a full key rotation across every service? That's weeks of developer time. The proxy is an insurance policy that's invisible when things go right and invaluable when they don't.
"We'll just block AI tools entirely."
Some organizations try this, and it works in highly regulated environments with strong endpoint controls. But for most, it creates shadow AI — developers using personal accounts, browser-based tools, or API keys that bypass corporate controls entirely. You end up with less visibility, not more. The alternative is governed access: approved tools, clear policies, and a proxy that enforces them. Let your teams use AI productively while maintaining the controls your security posture requires.
"We'll build something in-house."
That's a valid approach for organizations with deep platform engineering teams. The question is scope and maintenance: prompt injection techniques evolve weekly, new models launch monthly, and OWASP updates their LLM risk framework regularly. Building the initial proxy is achievable — keeping up with the threat landscape, maintaining detection signatures, and supporting every model API your teams adopt is the long-term cost. Most organizations find that's not where they want their security engineers focused.
🚀 Next Steps Close the deal ▼
1 AI Crisis Room Experience. A hands-on, simulated exercise that walks cross-functional teams through a realistic AI security incident — such as a prompt injection chain leading to data exfiltration. Designed for security, engineering, compliance, and leadership to participate together. Surfaces gaps in AI incident response readiness and builds organizational muscle for when a real event occurs.
2 Proof of Value. Deploy the proxy in monitor-only mode against 2-3 of their active AI use cases. No blocking, no disruption — just visibility. Let the data speak: what's being sent, to which models, by whom, and what's coming back.
3 Executive Briefing. Present what you've learned — from the Crisis Room, the Proof of Value, or both — to CISO/CTO. Lead with their data, not your product. A clear picture of their AI risk posture is more persuasive than any pitch deck.
